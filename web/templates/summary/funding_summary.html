Who funds our politics

The main aim of much of the money that is channeled into British politics is to influence decisions taken by government. The funding of political parties is no exception. Political parties’ expenditures are thought to have risen threefold since the 1970s, while membership of the parties has plummeted. Stepping into the gap are the trade unions, for Labour, and a limited number of very wealthy donors from business and finance in the Conservative Party’s case.

ANALYSIS: WHAT THE DATA SHOWS

Gifts from the tax avoidance industry

21 May 2015, by Spinwatch

The four global accountancy firms – PWC, KPMG, Deloitte and Ernst & Young, also known as the Big Four – have captured the British government. They are embedded in it, earning hundreds of millions of pounds a year in government business, advising on everything from tax law to privatisation programmes.

They also famously donate hundreds of thousands of pounds in staff time to political parties every year. This expertise is not for free. It is a way of currying favour.

The data shows that ahead of the 2010 general election, the Conservative Party’s ‘Implementation Team’ – under Francis Maude – received the following donations in the form of Big Four staff: £145k of KPMG’s time; £100k from PWC; £21k from Ernst & Young; and £9k from Deloitte.

Ahead of the 2015 election, Labour were the main beneficiaries: shadow Cabinet member Rachel Reeves took £100k of staff time from PWC in 2014; Tristram Hunt £75k; and David Hanson (shadow immigration minister) was given £66k.

At the same time, the Big Four are central to the tax avoidance industry. You work it out.

PROFILE

Peter Cruddas

Referred to as the City's richest self-made man, billionaire businessman Peter Cruddas, made his fortune in the City, founding spread-betting firm CMC Markets.

He first donated to the Conservative Party in 2009, gifting them £200k. In 2011 he gave a further £400k. His total donations amount to over £1m. He was appointed Conservative Party’s Co-Treasurer in 2011.

We have Cruddas to thank, partly, for the UK still having a first past the post voting system. In the 2015 general election this delivered results that saw a Tory MP needing just 34k votes to secure a seat, but UKIP and the Greens winning a single seat with 3.8m and 1.1m votes. This ‘democratic fraud’ could have been averted had the UK voted to adopt an Altervative Voting system in 2011. Cruddas gifted £400k to the No2AV campaign.

In 2012 Cruddas was embroiled in the Cash for Access scandal by undercover reporters from the Sunday Times. He informed the journalists, who said they from an overseas wealth fund, that large donations to his party would grant them access to the PM and Chancellor. He described donations of £250,000 as “premier league” and would allow donors a chance to influence government policy, before claiming that it would be “awesome for your business”.

The revelations forced Cruddas to immediately resign from his role as Party Treasurer. The Conservatives swiftly replaced him with major donor Lord Stanley Fink.
